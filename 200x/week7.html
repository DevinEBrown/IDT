Course Learning Outcomes vs. Learning Objectives

Course Learning Outcomes

Course learning outcomes are broad statements that describe the overall knowledge, skills, and attitudes that students are expected to gain by the end of a course. They are often more general and encompass the broader goals of the course. These outcomes provide a big-picture view of what students should be able to achieve as a result of completing the entire course.

Example 1
By the end of the course, students will possess skills in UI and UX design, allowing them to design intuitive interfaces and enhance user satisfaction through effective interaction web design

Example 2
By the end of the course, students will be able to develop and implement comprehensive digital marketing strategies to achieve organizational objectives.

Learning Objectives

Learning objectives are specific, measurable, and observable statements that detail the intended accomplishments of a lesson or instructional activity. They are more focused and provide a clear direction for educators and students regarding what is expected to be achieved in a particular lesson, module, or unit.

Example 1
By the end of the module, students should be able to explain user-centered design principles and how they contribute to creating a positive user experience on a website.

Example 2
By the end of the module, students should be able to explain the role of digital marketing in the overall marketing mix.

Bloom's Taxonomy

Bloom's Taxonomy is a hierarchical framework that classifies educational objectives from simple to complex. Benjamin Bloom and his colleagues developed it, and it consists of six levels, arranged in ascending order of complexity:

Remembering: Recalling or retrieving previously learned information.
Example: List the stages of the Successive Approximation Model (SAM).

Understanding:Comprehending and interpreting information
Example: Explain the concept of Successive Approximation Model (SAM) stages in your own words.

Applying: Applying acquired knowledge and skills in new situations.
Example: Use the Successive Approximation Model (SAM) to solve real-world problems.

Analyzing: Breaking down information into parts to understand its organizational structure.
Example: Compare and contrast the Successive Approximation Model (SAM) and the ADDIE Model .

Evaluating: Making judgments or assessments based on criteria and standards.
Example: Critique the effectiveness of the Successive Approximation Model (SAM).

Creating: Synthesizing information to produce new ideas or products
Example: Develop a course using the Successive Approximation Model (SAM).
