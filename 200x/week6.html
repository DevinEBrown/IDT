Week 6 | SAM & Instructional Design Model Choice

Successive Approximation Model (SAM)

Overview

The Successive Approximation Model (SAM) is an instructional design framework and process for creating effective and interactive learning experiences. It was developed by Michael W. Allen, an instructional designer, and is known for its iterative and collaborative approach to course development. SAM is designed to be more flexible and responsive than traditional linear instructional design models.

Phases

  1. Preparation: This phase involves establishing project goals, identifying the target audience, and defining the learning objectives. It also includes assembling the project team and determining the project's overall scope.
  2. Iterative Design: This phase of SAM, where rapid prototypes are created, reviewed, and refined in a series of cycles. Each cycle includes analysis, design, development, and evaluation steps.
  3. Iterative Development: This phase involves creating and refining rapid prototypes based on stakeholder feedback, fostering collaboration among team members, and repeating cycles of prototyping, review, and revision.

Implications For Instructional Design

The SAM significantly impacts instructional design, promoting an iterative and collaborative process. Its flexibility allows for continuous refinement based on stakeholder feedback. For instance, in e-learning, SAM permits rapid prototyping of modules, enabling timely adjustments. The model's Agile principles facilitate responsiveness to evolving content, which is crucial for industries with rapidly changing information, like technology or healthcare.

SAM's emphasis on active stakeholder involvement transforms the design landscape. In corporate training, regular client reviews during SAM's iterative cycles ensure that the learning modules align precisely with organizational goals. This collaborative engagement fosters a sense of ownership among stakeholders, enhancing the overall effectiveness of the instructional design process. SAM's approach has especially gained traction in healthcare education, where updates to medical practices require swift incorporation into training materials, ensuring healthcare professionals are always equipped with the latest knowledge.

Strengths and Limitations

Strengths:
The SAM Model excels in its iterative design, allowing constant refinement and adaptation. Its flexibility accommodates evolving content, making it ideal for dynamic subject matter. SAM's emphasis on stakeholder collaboration fosters engagement and ensures alignment with learning objectives.

Limitations:
The SAM Model's iterative nature may extend project timelines, potentially conflicting with strict deadlines. The need for consistent stakeholder involvement can be resource-intensive. In cases where a linear, well-defined process is essential, SAM's flexibility may seem overwhelming. Additionally, successful implementation of SAM requires a skilled and cohesive project team, and organizations unfamiliar with iterative design might find it challenging to transition from traditional models.
