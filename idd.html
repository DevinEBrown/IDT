Instructional Design Document


Mini-Course/Course Title

Building User-Centric Websites

Course Overview

Building User-Centric Websites course is designed to introduce learners to the principles and practices of web design. This Mini-Course aims to introduce responsive web design, equipping learners with the skills to create websites that adapt to different screens and devices.

Learning/Knowledge Gap

One specific learning gap that might be addressed in this course is understanding the importance of accessibility in web design. This includes ensuring that websites are usable and navigable by individuals with disabilities, such as those who use screen readers or have other accessibility needs. Learners may not be familiar with web accessibility, so incorporating this topic into the curriculum would be valuable in creating more inclusive and user-friendly websites.

Target Audience

Demographics

Grade: 9 - 12
Education: Basic familiarity with computers and the internet, but no prior formal education in web design.

Background

Learners in grades 9 - 12 will likely have some experience using computers and the internet for basic tasks such as browsing, social media, and school assignments. They may have some exposure to simple website navigation but are not expected to have any formal training in web design.

Skills and Dispositions

Skills
  • Basic computer literacy: Familiarity with operating systems (Windows or macOS) and common software and applications (Google Drive, web browsers).
  • Proficiency in using the internet for research and communication.
  • Basic understanding of file management and organization.
Dispositions
  • Curiosity and an interest in digital technology.
  • Eagerness to learn new skills related to web design.

Learners in grades 9 - 12 are at an age where they are starting to develop a more sophisticated understanding of technology and its applications. They will likely be motivated by creative projects and interested in how websites work.

Course Type

Step-by-Step Instructional Course

A step-by-step instructional course provides students with a clear and structured learning path. This approach effectively introduces and reinforces concepts systematically, ensuring that students build a strong foundation in web design and accessibility.

Course Modality

Blended Learning

Blended Learning, which combines online instruction with in-person activities or discussions, is well-suited for this mini-course. It allows for a balance between structured online lessons and interactive in-person activities. Given that the target audience is in grades 9 -12, a blended approach recognizes their familiarity with digital tools while providing valuable face-to-face interactions for discussions, collaboration, and hands-on assistance. This modality ensures that learners can access course materials conveniently while still benefiting from the social and collaborative aspects of in-person learning.

Course Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course, students will be able to:

  1. Explain the fundamental principles of web design, web design layout, color schemes, and typography.
  2. Apply web design tools and software to construct functional and visually appealing web pages.
  3. Identify web accessibility and implement best practices to create user-friendly, inclusive websites.
  4. Evaluate and analyze existing websites for accessibility issues and propose effective improvement solutions.
  5. Create a user-centric website, ensuring the incorporation of accessibility features for an inclusive user experience.

Module

Introduction to Typography

Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, students will be able to:

  1. Define typography and its role in web design.
    aligns to course learning outcome (CLO) (1,3)
  2. Categorize typefaces into major classifications (serif, sans-serif, script, etc.) and explain the characteristics that define each category.
    aligns to course learning outcome (CLO) (1)
  3. Evaluate best practices of typography in responsive web design.
    aligns to course learning outcome (CLO) (3,4)

Lesson

Understanding Typography in Web Design

Learning Activities

Activity 1: Introduction
This activity is lecture focused and will begin with a brief discussion on the importance of visual appeal in web design and how typography plays a crucial role. The lecture will define typography and emphasize that it involves selecting typefaces, classification, hierarchy, font sizes, line lengths, and other design elements to convey a specific message. We will also discuss the impact of typography on user experience and how it can influence the perception of a website.
aligns to module learning outcome (MLO) (1, 2, 3)

Activity 2: Typography Exploration
In small groups, students will explore the website. They will analyze the typography used on these websites. Groups will take note of the typefaces, font sizes, line spacing, and any other typographic elements that stand out to them. After exploration, each group presents their findings, discussing how the typography contributes to the overall design and user experience.
aligns to module learning outcome (MLO) (1, 2, 3)

Assessment Strategies

Assessment 1: Interactive Quiz
This interactive quiz will assess students' understanding of key typography concepts, including the definition of typography, the impact of typography on user experience, and the terminology associated with typography (e.g., serif, sans-serif, leading, kerning).
aligns to module learning outcome (MLO) (1, 2)

Assessment 2: Typography in Action Assignment
In this assignment, students will create a simple webpage with typography. Students will experiment with different typefaces, hierarchies, font sizes, and colors to convey moods or messages. Students will also present and discuss the impact of their typographic choices on the overall design and message of their typography webpage.
aligns to module learning outcome (MLO) (3)

Instructional Design Model

Using the Successive Approximation Model (SAM) for the minicourse is strategic due to its iterative and agile nature. SAM allows for continuous refinement, prototyping, and collaboration, aligning seamlessly with the dynamic nature of web design. The development process becomes responsive to learner feedback by employing SAM, ensuring that the course content evolves iteratively. This approach enables the creation of early prototypes, fostering hands-on, practical learning experiences for high school students. SAM's emphasis on formative assessment and real-world application ensures the minicourse remains engaging, effective, and tailored to the evolving needs of the target audience.

As a UX designer, SAM is a beneficial approach because it emphasizes iterative prototyping and user feedback throughout the design process, allowing for continuous refinement and adaptation to learner needs, resulting in a more student-centric final course.

Learning Theory

The learning theories best used in this course are Constructivism and Connectivism. Constructivism emphasizes active learning through hands-on experiences. Collaborative web design projects, design critiques, and problem-solving scenarios engage students, fostering a deeper understanding of web design principles. Connectivism views learning as network-distributed, emphasizing technology and social connections. Activities like collaborative online tools, peer reviews, and web accessibility audits leverage digital platforms, encouraging students to tap into online resources for a networked and socially connected learning experience.
